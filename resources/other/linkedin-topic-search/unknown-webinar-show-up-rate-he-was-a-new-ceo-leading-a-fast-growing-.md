He was a new CEO leading a fast-growing company, but couldn't remember the last time he'd felt like himself.

One of my clients felt like he wasn't living up to the quality of work the founder used to do. 

He felt unnatural when delivering webinars, under-resourced for the ambitious goals they had set, and like he was failing on every front.

When we finally sat down together, he said, "Something feels off. I can't name it. But I'm not myself. I don't even feel like I have time to check in with myself on how I'm doing day to day."

As we unpacked what was happening, something came into focus.

He was comparing himself to the founder in everything he did. In team meetings, he'd wonder why people reacted differently to him. In emails to his audience, he'd question why open rates were down or why webinar show-up rate had dropped.

"Is it something about me? Is there something fundamentally broken about my leadership? Am I not cut out for this job?"

He said it out loud. And as he did, the spiral he was in came into view.

He wasn't just burnt out. He was chasing a leadership style that wasn't his.

And in the process, he'd stopped doing everything that used to keep him whole. 

No workouts. No hikes. Poor sleep. Quick meals at his desk.

When I asked what he used to do that helped him feel most like himself, he paused.

"Long hikes. No phone. Just me and the dogs."

At our next session, he came back smiling after a long hike with his dog. 

One hike didn't fix everything.

But it gave him a practice to return to and a center to rebuild from. That was enough to shift his capacity and allow him to start leading with more authenticity and vulnerability.

The tendency when you're in a downward spiral is to grind it out. But those are often the exact moments when returning to what restores you matters most.
