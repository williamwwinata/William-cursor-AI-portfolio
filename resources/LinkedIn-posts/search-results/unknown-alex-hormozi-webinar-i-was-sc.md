- 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗔𝗹𝗲𝘅 𝗛𝗼𝗿𝗺𝗼𝘇𝗶 𝗾𝘂𝗼𝘁𝗲 𝘁𝗵𝗮𝘁 𝗺𝗮𝗱𝗲 𝗺𝗲 𝘀𝗮𝘆 '𝗢𝘂𝗰𝗵' -

I was scrolling through social media when I came across a quote from Alex Hormozi that hit me:

"Learning only begins when there are new behaviours under the same conditions.” 

Ouch. 

It forced me to confront an uncomfortable truth about my own behaviour.

I have been learning voraciously for years. 
Financial planning strategies, estate planning techniques or coaching methodologies via books, courses, podcasts and webinars? Done it all.

Each new piece of knowledge felt important and valuable for my professional development.

But as I sat there reflecting on Hormozi's words, I had to admit something painful.

I have been guilty of learning too much and not executing enough changes.

The problem was not that I did not value implementation. I genuinely wanted to apply everything I was learning.

But by wanting to apply everything, I was drowning.

Drowning in a sea of important insights that tore me in different directions.

Here is what I discovered:
The "implement everything important" belief was actually preventing me from implementing anything effectively.

When everything important feels urgent to implement simultaneously, it becomes overwhelming to even begin anywhere.

So instead of choosing imperfect action in one area, I defaulted back to learning more.

When I looked back at the most significant insights I had discovered, I counted at least five major breakthroughs that could each transform my business effectiveness.

But trying to implement all five simultaneously meant making progress on none of them.

The breakthrough came when I reframed the question entirely.

Instead of wondering "How can I implement all these important insights?" I asked "𝗪𝗵𝗶𝗰𝗵 𝗼𝗻𝗲 𝗼𝗿 𝘁𝘄𝗼 𝗶𝗻𝘀𝗶𝗴𝗵𝘁𝘀, 𝘄𝗵𝗲𝗻 𝗶𝗺𝗽𝗹𝗲𝗺𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗲𝗱 𝗱𝗲𝗲𝗽𝗹𝘆 𝗳𝗼𝗿 𝟵𝟬 𝗱𝗮𝘆𝘀, 𝘄𝗼𝘂𝗹𝗱 𝗰𝗿𝗲𝗮𝘁𝗲 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗺𝗼𝘀𝘁 𝗶𝗺𝗽𝗮𝗰𝘁?"

This shift from comprehensive implementation to focused execution changed everything.

The most successful professionals are not those who know the most or learn the fastest.

They are the ones who identify key leverage points and execute on them consistently.

The hard lesson learnt throughout the years was… 𝗹𝗲𝗮𝗿𝗻𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝘄𝗶𝘁𝗵𝗼𝘂𝘁 𝗲𝘅𝗲𝗰𝘂𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗶𝘀 𝗷𝘂𝘀𝘁 𝗲𝘅𝗽𝗲𝗻𝘀𝗶𝘃𝗲 𝗲𝗻𝘁𝗲𝗿𝘁𝗮𝗶𝗻𝗺𝗲𝗻𝘁.

So here is my question for all of us: 
“What is the gap between what we have learnt and what we have actually put into practice? 
More importantly, what are we going to do about it?"
