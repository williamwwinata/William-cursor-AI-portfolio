I've been stuck on a positioning problem for at least a year. Most people come to eWebinar, and they don't immediately get our value prop.

On the other side, we have raving fans and a big enough customer base to tell us we could be a great business. But something feels stuck.

I've been so frustrated with not being able to figure out the answer. What else could we be, if not an automated webinar?!

Last week, I interviewed Chris Walker for ProfitLed Season 3 (coming to you soon), and our conversation made me question how I've been defining my business.

We talked about the power of reframing who you believe you are.

Most of us accept our behaviors as fixed parts of our personality. "I'm a reactive person." "I'm not a morning person." We say these things so often they become who we are.

What if instead of "I'm a reactive person," I said, "I'm a person who has been expressing reactively"? The first version is a permanent identity. The second is a pattern that can be broken.

💡That's when it hit me: I've been doing the same thing with my business.

My first two startups were blue ocean companies. I know the pain of educating a market from scratch too well. With eWebinar, I deliberately chose a "purple ocean" strategy: take a concept that already exists and introduce it to a different audience.

That framing shaped our website, our messaging, our content. All of it assumed people already understood what an automated webinar was.

The problem is most people are problem aware, not solution aware. Doing live webinars has been the norm for so long that most people haven't thought to solve it.

I was so focused on not wanting another blue ocean product that I locked it into a purple ocean frame and never questioned it. That meant we could only speaking to people who already had context.

But introducing an existing concept to an audience who has never heard of it is still blue ocean. For them! My refusal to accept that was the thing keeping me stuck.

I asked myself, if I reframe eWebinar as a blue ocean product, how would I approach it differently?

Instead of leading with benefits, I would first help people understand the limitations of what they're doing today. Instead of assuming they see the problem, I would prove it exists. Then, once they agree it's worth solving, introduce how we address it.

Same product. Same value. Completely different starting point.

We have yet to implement this, but it already feels like a huge unlock.

If you feel stuck and can't figure out why, try questioning the thing you've accepted as fact. The assumption you've never challenged because you decided it was true a long time ago.

Your identity is not your behavior. Your product is not its label. The thing keeping you stuck might just be the frame you put around it.
