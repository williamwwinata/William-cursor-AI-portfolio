Starting my marketing growth agency was the worst and best decision I made in 2026.

Here's why.

Back in 2022, I worked as a funnel builder inside a marketing agency. Seven of us. We grew a single offer to over $48 million in total revenue in 3 years.

I was in the room. I watched it happen. I helped execute the strategies that got us there. That period taught me more about marketing than anything else I'd ever done.

So naturally, the thought hit me - if I start my own agency and apply everything I learned, I can replicate those results for my clients.

What I didn't fully understand then was this: that level of success wasn't just strategy. It was leverage.

The client had ad budgets north of $100k every single week. We had a full team of specialists, each owning their lane. 

The founder had a massive organic following and was a genuinely compelling speaker. Every piece was already in place before we even touched it.

Fast forward to agency life — and I'm now working with businesses that have none of that. No ad spend. Weak organic reach. 

Founders who can barely read off a teleprompter. And they want the same results they got sold on the discovery call.

It's not possible. Not with that setup.

So we had to get creative about what leverage actually looks like when you don't have a $400k payroll or a celebrity founder.

One of my recent clients is a good example. He wasn't willing to spend on ads. But he had a community I'd helped grow from zero to 300+ active paid members. We were launching his first-ever high-ticket offer - teaching people how to start an AI marketing business.

We went back to fundamentals. Went deep on world-class copywriting frameworks. Used the Jason Fladlien webinar structure, refined the script with AI, and built everything around his organic channel. 

He wasn't a natural presenter, so we ran multiple dry runs until he got comfortable.

The webinar launched with a 35%+ show rate. 18 calls booked. $44k in contracted revenue. $22k cash collected within five days.
His first time. Ever.

The difference wasn't a massive budget. It was applying the same leverage principles that worked at $48 million, adapted to where he actually was.

Success is still about fundamentals. The fundamentals don't change. Your access to them does.
