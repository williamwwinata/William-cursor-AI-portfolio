A few years ago, I was in a meeting with one of my clients (you probably know him) 

We were talking about closes and angles he could use when wrapping up his webinars.

So I'm sitting there brainstorming with him... throwing out ideas...

And then I came up with something we ended up calling the fire hose close.

He made a note of it. Used it in his upcoming webinars. Made a lot of money with it.

Fast forward years later...

Someone comes up to me at an event.

He goes, "You gave us the fire hose close."

And I'm like... what fire hose? I didn't even remember it.

And he says:

Imagine walking down the street holding a fire hose… and you see a house on fire.

That's what's happening right now…

You're in the right place, at the right time.

That was the close.

And this is what we call a laid out.

One simple image that reframed the decision so clearly… saying "no" felt irrational.

You've got the solution. The problem is right in front of you. What are you gonna do… keep walking?

NO… you're going to do something about it, which at the time was investing in whatever I'm selling.

That's why it's still being used today.

I didn't pull it from some swipe file I'd been building. It wasn't a pre-written close I'd been working on. And it definitely wasn't some clever copywriting flex.

It was one metaphor.

And that's the only reason it still works.

Because here's what most people don't realize about metaphors...

They're not just a "nice to have" in your copy.

They're one of the most powerful selling tools you can use.

You've probably heard the saying… "a confused prospect doesn't buy."

Well guess what?

Metaphors are the fastest way to turn someone from a confused prospect to a crystal clear one.

A good metaphor takes something complex, abstract, or scary… and turns it into something so familiar… the brain stops fighting it.

So here's how I think about creating metaphors:

First… you don't start with the product. You start with the confusion.

What is the audience struggling to understand? Where do they keep getting stuck? What feels abstract, risky, or hard to evaluate?

Then you ask yourself a simple question: "What is this already like?"

Not in marketing terms. In real life. Everyday situations. Physical experiences. Moments people have already lived through.

The closer the metaphor is to something they've personally experienced… the faster it works.

That's also why the best metaphors don't feel clever. They feel obvious.

And that's the goal.

If your metaphor makes people think… "Oh… yeah. That makes sense."

You've already won.

Because once they understand the situation clearly… the offer becomes the natural next step.

Metaphors don't decorate the sale.

They ARE the sale.
