Now - why is PLG so damn hard? (Looks like I'm only talking hard things about hard thing recently 😅) Before we start dunking on PLG-tech companies for not implementing *all* the PLG best practices - let's invite our good old friend Nuance back into our lives. 

Here's a more critical breakdown on *why* even PLG-tech companies don't implement all of the PLG practices, and why is PLG so damn hard, but totally worth it: 

🟢 1)  first - not every product lends itself to the PLG model. Now, I said that. If they have high setup or running costs, and wanted to embrace a freemium model, which has the lowest conversion to paid from all models, you'd need to impose extremely stringent usage limits on your free accounts. Oftentimes, these limits would have to be too low for the users to experience the value of your product. So instead of being an acquisition engine, the free tier would just be a cost hog and even possibly an acquisition deterrent. Same with extremely complex/technical products that may require a few weeks to set up and benefit from. Would a 2-week trial make sense then? 

🟢 2) BUT - that *doesn't* mean that the companies who don't implement freemium/free trial are *not* PLG! You can have a sales-led acquisition model and still drive expansion revenue through your product (= generate Product-Led Growth!) 

🟢 3) Why is PLG so hard to implement though? 

 🟥 It's effort-intensive, especially at the beginning: PLG, like SEO, requires significant upfront effort to reach its "evergreen" benefits. While it promises self-serve, cost-effective, and continuous user activation and conversion, reaching this stage demands time and hard work.

 🟥 Slow Growth Initially: PLG companies tend to grow slower than sales-led companies, especially in the beginning. This can lead to pressure from stakeholders to abandon the PLG approach.

 🟥  Had to get right, so so easy to get wrong: If you ask people to tell you about a self-serve product they've used with a bad onboarding experience - you'll see a show of hands. Ask them about a freemium product that gave them an *amazing* onboarding experience - and you'll likely hear crickets. 
It's because a truly great self-serve onboarding requires a lot of thought and planning. We're revamping our own free trial onboarding experience now - and we're already 8 weeks into the process 🤯 So no wonder a lot of companies get it wrong.

🟥  No one to own it at an early-stage: In early-stage companies, the product team is often stretched thin delivering new releases, making it hard for them to focus on improving user experience and feature adoption.
