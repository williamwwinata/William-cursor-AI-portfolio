I was forced to bootstrap because VCs wouldn't fund me. Profitability gave me ultimate freedom, my no.1 priority in life. I knew I'd never build another company any other way.

I moved to NYC to build my last startup. That city made me feel like I needed venture capital in order to have permission to succeed. I was struggling to get my last startup off the ground and did what everyone else was doing -- getting intros and pitching VCs using a generic pitch deck template.

The problem was, my product wasn't venture scalable and building a unicorn wasn't what I authentically wanted. I just needed money because I was in so much debt; I can't think of a worse reason to raise capital.

I was telling a story that I was taught to tell rather than one I believed in. I told it so much that I started to sometimes believe it. To keep my company going, I kept iterating on the product and closing deals when I could, and eventually broke even on our very lean team.

As a last ditch effort to join the venture club just for the stamp of approval, I even joined an accelerator...but then quit in the first week because I realized all they wanted to do was to take 5% for almost no money, get us "demo day ready" so they could sell more of our equity for a higher valuation. I couldn't live with giving away any percentage for a company I fought to save from the dead. Luckily, I made that decision, because that startup was acquired a year later. That 5% would've been painful to give away.

Through this experience which lasted 2 years, I learned that the only people who have to give you money are your customers. 

I learned that the value you get from having 100% freedom to make choices for your company that you think about 24/7 is priceless.

I learned that there is so much more to building companies than revenue. The most important thing in this journey is the life you get to live EVERY SINGLE DAY.
