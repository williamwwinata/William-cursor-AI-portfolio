I wanted to sell my last startup because I was no longer having fun - this is a common reason that doesn't get talked about. 😶 

After 10 years in the same industry (real estate), I was ready for a change. Sales were hard because our audience resisted technology; revenue opportunities were limited and support was soul crushing. I woke up every day hating my job, hating my life.

There were so many cool things happening in the world that I wasn't a part of. The opportunity cost of not doing something different was getting bigger and bigger.

I couldn't tell my cofounder because I didn't want him to be demotivated too. We worked together for a decade and never saw success the way we wanted. Most days, it felt like we were trucking along.

I only ever paid myself 50% of market salary at our peak, and him 75%. I didn't see a light at the end of the tunnel. Things were improving, but too slowly.

One day, I called up an industry friend and mentor to vent. His name was Aaron, Founder of HomeSpotter. "I wonder if my cofounder would let me sell my shares so I can move on."

"If you're serious, we're looking to make our first acquisition. But you're going to have to stay for two years." He said. I never thought about selling before that.

3 things were important to me in an acquisition deal:
1. A CEO I respected and would be happy working for.
2. A well-loved product that I would be proud of associating my own with.
3. A company that would embrace my nomadic lifestyle and take care of my team.

He made an offer a week later. I knew him for a number of years, enough to know that that was the best offer he was able to make. He was one of the few people I'd consider working for.

Price was not a primary factor so I didn't shop the company. I also didn't want to put on a dog and pony show and pretend that I was excited about growing the company that I had grown to resent. Aaron knew my heart wasn't in it, but he also knew that I would always show up for our customers.

Two months later, my startup, Spacio, was acquired by HomeSpotter, and Aaron became my CEO.

You don't have to keep pushing because it's the "right thing to do". You don't have to sell to the highest bidder. Sometimes, you just need to move on for your own sanity.

Another two months after that in 2019, I started eWebinar because I needed a new challenge and distraction. And because I didn't sell Spacio for "retirement level money", I knew I needed to get going with something else if I don't want to work forever.

When you say no to something that doesn't bring you joy, you're giving yourself space to say yes to something else that will.
