I used to tell everyone I meet what I was doing with my startup. I thought I was doing it to validate ideas and get clarity on execution.

As I pitched more, I noticed something shift. Instead of listening for feedback, I would often get defensive. I found myself trying to prove myself instead of having a real conversation.

I’m sure that was pretty annoying for them. Nobody wants to be around someone who’s always pitching them. I don’t either.

I asked myself why I was doing this?

Truth was, I wasn’t confident enough in my own decisions. I was seeking external validation because I often second guessed myself.

You can look outside yourself to validate an idea or get support. But you cannot look outside yourself to feel good about yourself. Confidence comes from within.

Here’s what I’ve learned: not everyone is going to understand what you do. And not everyone has to understand for you to be doing the right thing.

Most successful founders will tell you that in the beginning, everyone thought they were crazy. It’s like that quote, “First they think you’re crazy, then they fight you, then you change the world.”

One of the superpowers of being a founder is having conviction in your own idea regardless of what other people say. It’s being able to listen to different versions of advice, take it with a grain of salt, and still act with total confidence on the idea and execution you believe in.

If everyone thinks it’s a good idea, everyone would be doing it.

💡 It’s because they don’t see your vision and you do, that you have the opportunity to create something unique.

There’s a lot of people building in public right now, and there’s a certain danger to that. If you post something out there, you might get a peanut gallery telling you to do something different.

It’s very easy for someone to tell you how to do something better when they’ve never done it themselves. People love giving unsolicited advice. They’re not the ones thinking about your business 24/7. They just drop a comment and leave.

When I realized I seeking validation because I lacked conviction, I stopped feeling the need to over share. It was more distracting than helpful. Instead, I learned to listen to my own intuition and my own voice.

I don't take advice from someone who hasn’t been I am and hasn’t gone where I want to go.

If I want feedback, I go to people I respect. I listen more than I speak. I take it for what it is: advice.

In the end, you are the only person who has to live with the consequences of your own decisions. Which means you are the only person who can choose something that you will not regret doing.

Here’s the thing about choices: you can always make them over and over and over again. You are your own biggest safety net.
