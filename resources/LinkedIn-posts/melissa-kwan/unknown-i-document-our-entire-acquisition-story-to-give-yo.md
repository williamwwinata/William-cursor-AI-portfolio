I document our entire acquisition story to give you a glimpse into the hidden process behind what happens when a startup gets acquired, from offer to close. ✍ 

When I sold my last company, I had no idea what I was doing. Our acquirer even had to tell me that entering into negotiations meant that we were, "In a process."

I didn't shop the company as one would expect in order to extract the highest dollar.

I was having a casual catch up conversation with a friend, CEO/founder of a mid-sized startup, and mentioned I felt ready for a new challenge after spending a decade in real estate tech. I had a good business, but our product didn't have market potential for it ever to be a great business. I saw so many cool things happening in the world that I wasn't a part of, and the opportunity cost of not trying something else was weighing on me.

That casual call turned into more serious discussions about what it could be like to merge our companies; where our product could be a nice addition to their existing suite of solutions.

I thought about all the things that were important to me, like preserving the team, staying remote, and getting market salaries. I thought about the kind of person I'd want to work for after being my own boss for almost a decade. Together, we came up with a valuation and set of terms that was fair and maximized motivation on both sides.

After the LOI, followed two grueling months of due diligence, emotional roller coaster, and an incident where the deal almost didn't close because I couldn't locate a shareholder; a publicly traded company who had inherited shares from a dev shop we traded work for equity with. ( 💌 PS. Never, ever, ever trade work for equity.)

We had a two month timeline to close because there was a conference we wanted to announce our merger at, which was a hyper-ambitious goal given year end holidays. But, we did it - for the first time in a long time, I felt like I could breathe.

While it wasn’t the financial outcome I hoped it would be, it was the best outcome I could’ve asked for. We found the perfect home with a company that appreciated and respected our team and the product we built.

We’re made to believe startup exits should be in the hundreds of millions. But most aren’t the kind you read about in the media. They’re not retirement-level payouts, but still life-changing for a founder like me who was drowning in debt, paying herself less than $50k a year, and had no disposable income to live "normally".
