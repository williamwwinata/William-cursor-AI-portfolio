The first boss I ever had told me that I didn't know everything, and that changed the trajectory of my life forever. 🚀 

I was a late bloomer because my parents were overly protective. I wasn't allowed to have my a job until I graduated at 21. They wanted me to focus in school without any distractions, something they never had the luxury to do. I did well and was always top of class (or close to). I was self motivated. I worked hard. I was a good kid. 

Growing up, my parents told me I could do anything! I lived the first half of my life with the deluded idea that I actually could do anything, even though I hadn't developed any skills.

In my first job as a receptionist at real estate marketing firm, I met the first person I admired professionally. Her name was Lily. She was a cofounder to the company, spoke 6 languages, and could sell anything. Everyone loved her. I aspired to be only a fraction of her when I grew up.

Because of my deluded sense of self, I thought I knew more than I did. I went into that job thinking it would be easy. I was a receptionist! How hard could it be?

On my first day, when the phone rang, I picked it up and said, "Hello?" as if I was answering it at home. I didn't even know how to answer the phone properly. Lily had to teach me everything, starting with a script taped in front of me, "Hello, Melissa speaking. How may I help you?" I remember my phone voice as if it was yesterday.

One day, Lily was teaching me something new and I questioned her approach. She looked at me dead in the eye and said, "Melissa, you don't know everything."

It struck me. My arrogance shone through to the person I respected the most in the world. I felt ashamed and disappointed, and promised myself that it would never happen again.

In life, you can be a teacher, or a student. That was the moment I walked to the other side of the room and became a student. I started listening instead of talking. Learning instead of teaching. Asking questions instead of answering them.

💡 I became teachable. 

The more I knew, the more I didn't know. My mind started expanding, my network started growing, and opportunities started presenting themselves. "You don't know everything" opened up my entire world.

Our biggest challenge in building a startup is not product, sales, or marketing. It's finding great people; A-players who inspire each other. If I think back to the ones who didn't work out on our team, it's teachability. If I think about the ones we never want to lose, it's also teachability.

In life, you can be a student, or a teacher.
