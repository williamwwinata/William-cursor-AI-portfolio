As founders, we focus so much on our work lives that we often forget our personal lives are happening in parallel.

We drown ourselves in content around struggles and successes. Obsess over revenue milestones, growth strategies, and the AI-craze. It’s all we consume to the point where it’s nauseating.

I've been that founder who skipped one of her best friend's weddings because she could only justify spending money on business trips. Who worked through every weekend. Who told herself she'd travel later, date later, live later.

We do this to ourselves because we live in the future. We tell ourselves that delayed gratification is the price of admission for survival.

💡 Plot twist: life doesn't wait for your startup to succeed.

Life happens while you're building. And if you don't pay attention, it'll just pass you by.

We need to live. Go on dates. Travel. See our friends. Do all the things that make life worth living. Not after we've made it. Right now.

It's one thing to feel like you're not moving fast enough professionally. It's another to feel like you're falling behind in your personal life too. That's the kind of thing regret is made of.

We're all progressing in our personal lives. We just don't give it weight.

I lived this. For a decade.

I was so deep in startup mode that I couldn't see how much my life was expanding around me. I was meeting the love of my life, building lifelong friendships, discovering cities that would shape who I was becoming. All of it was happening and yet I wasn't present enough to appreciate it.

It wasn't until I sold my last company six years ago that I had a moment to catch my breath. When I looked back at those years, I was overcome with gratitude. Not by the business outcomes, but the life outcomes.

None of it was part of my business plan.

Building a startup is hard. It's soul-crushing. We don't always get back what we put in, and that's what makes it so omnipresent. Our startup is one part of our life, and even though it's an important part, it's not the entire part.

It's not the founder journey that matters most. It's not the revenue, the milestones, or even the exit. It's the people you meet along the way. The cities you get to discover. The chaos that happens as a result of your hustle. Everything that makes a great story later that you forgot to laugh about while it was happening.

What matters most is the human journey your startup takes you on. That's the whole picture.

So don't forget to zoom out. Stop and smell the flowers. Take that trip. Go to the wedding. Pay yourself. Say yes to the date. Celebrate the life you're in.

This is it. Nothing happens next. Don't miss it.
