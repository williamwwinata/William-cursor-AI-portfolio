10 years ago today, I moved to NYC and triggered the butterfly effect that led to the sale of my last company and the beginning of my current one.

I was 32 and a struggling startup founder. I asked a friend what the best thing he did for his career was. He said, "When I was in my 20s, I had $600 in my pocket and I moved to NYC." I moved there a month later from Vancouver.

When I got there, I had the opportunity to join a co-living space. It was a 5 bedroom, 2-level townhouse in the middle of the city. It was a place where we could live, work, and host events. A spot where we could build community in both our personal and professional lives. I sold my car and instead of paying off my debts, I invested in this project. 

I met David at this house, my life partner and cofounder to eWebinar. He was our first "Airbnb guest" who sublet my roommate's room.

In the living room of this house was where I met every new friend who introduced me to business connections. Those conversations taught me how to hone in on my startup idea.

Having a dining table that fit 18 people was how I was able to host a dinner with proptech industry leaders, including the CEO of Zillow. My peers started to notice me after that event. They became my friends, gave me advice, and helped me get to first revenue. That night was when I met Aaron, who would acquire my startup 4 years later. 

Eventually, David and I left New York to nomad full time for 3 years. Because of him, I got to experience the world, reinvent myself, and build a life around who I am today.

People sometimes say to me, "You can only bootstrap and live comfortably because you had an exit." Yes, that is true. But that was not the case for my first decade as an entrepreneur.

I moved to New York at the lowest point of my career.

I didn't have more than $100 in my account for two years. I had one meal a day and timed it at 4:30pm so I wouldn't get hungry at night.

I paid everyone else before paying myself rent and basic expenses depending on how much was left in our account. I maxed out every loan and credit card. My parents stopped talking to me after bailing me out multiple times.

I went to sleep with fear and woke up with hustle.

💡Here's the thing: I loved every moment of it because it gave me the adventure of a lifetime.

Building a startup is hard. It's soul-crushing. You don't always get back what you put in.

As I think about how I moved to NYC on a whim, I'm reminded that the rewards of building a startup is not always the financial outcome at the end. Sometimes, like in my case, it's the human journey your company takes you on that is the most life-changing.

You never know if your butterfly effect is just around the corner.
