I'm anti-VC, but there are unexpected benefits from pitching to them. It's how we found product-market fit in my last startup; the best thing that came out of multiple rejections.
 
I was broke, in debt, and desperate to find someone who'd give me a check for survival. I pitched anyone who'd give me a meeting, VC firms, accelerators, angel networks, startup competitions. I can't think of a worse place for a founder to be in.

What no one tells you is that VCs say a lot of sh*t 💩, but outright rejection is not one of them.

This means that you could be constantly getting false positives from VCs who are “somewhat interested if only you were farther along”. This leads you to believe you might be onto something, even when you’re not. I also fell into this trap until I finally realized what was happening.

Some real-life examples: 🙃

👉 Me: What stage of companies do you invest in?
Pre-pitch: We invest in startups at all stages.
Post-pitch: You’re too early for us, but keep me posted on your progress. (Translation: No thanks, but let’s stay on good terms.)

👉 Me: How much traction do you need to see before investing?
Pre-pitch: It doesn’t matter, we can come in pretty early.
Post-pitch: We’d like to see more traction first. Who else are you talking to? (Translation: No thanks, but I’m a sheep with FOMO.)

👉 Me: What do you think about our product/business?
Pre-pitch: It’s interesting.
Post-pitch: It’s interesting. (Translation: It’s not that interesting.)

Learning how to pitch by putting together a deck, refining our elevator pitch, and doing extensive market and competitor research forced me to evaluate my pre-revenue business more quick and effectively than consumer research ever did.

We only made our first $10 after 2.5 years of pivoting because of a single piece of feedback a VC gave me before I left the room.
