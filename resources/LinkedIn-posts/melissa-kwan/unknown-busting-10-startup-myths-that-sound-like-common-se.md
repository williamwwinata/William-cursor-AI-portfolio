Busting 10 startup myths that sound like common sense: 👊

1. VC investment increases your chance of success.
Taking VC means you're committing to one kind of success: venture style returns with a unicorn exit. Not taking VC means you get to define success however you want. Success is self-defined as what you want.

2. You can only hire A-players if you pay the most.
A-players in startups are jack-of-all-trades who are smart, curious, fast learners, team players, passionate, self-motivated, value-aligned, not only money-driven, etc. Being the most skilled and senior doesn't make someone an A-player.

3. A CTO will join you if you give enough equity.
50% of $0 is $0. The best CTOs are constantly getting pitched. You need to convince them why you're the best person and idea they should give up everything else for. You are the opportunity cost they need to justify.

4. The bigger the TAM, the better the business.
Every business starts with dominating a niche before expanding. If you're something for everyone, you're nothing for anyone. You can do anything once you have a loyal group of customers.

5. Cofounders should have equal equity split.
Businesses are not democracies. Within a business, there are a number of responsibilities that need to be divided up, and equity split should be proportionate to those.

6. Hockey stick growth is your end goal. 🙄 
The idea that if you only find that one inflection point and your business will exponentially accelerate is unicorn porn made up by the industry. Significant growth is achieved by compounded efforts over time.

7. You'll sell your SaaS for 10x revenue.
Company valuations are dependent on a number of things like your product, industry, customers, revenue, profit, growth rate, operational health, strategic potential, you, team...list goes on. Just because you're a SaaS company, doesn't automatically give you 10x valuation. Do you know how hard it is to find someone willing to write a check to take over your business?

8. Measure everything or you're blind.
There'll come a time when measuring things become important. You need a big enough customer base to have meaningful data. Listening to the voice of your customers is enough to help create an awesome product they'll rave about in the earlier days.

9. You need to be the market leader.
The pie is big enough that everyone can have a slice. You can have a great business if you create value for a subset of customers. Products need competition to differentiate and consumers need choices.

10. It's a lonely journey.
You're only alone if you never choose to share your burdens. With so many communities at our fingertips now, peer support has never been more accessible.
