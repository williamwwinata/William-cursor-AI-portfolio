I used to think founders should get paid last and the least. Now, I'm in the opposite camp.💰

“But it’s our dream! We need to sacrifice for our vision to succeed!” - Said us.

I had 2 startups before eWebinar. I only got a real salary in year 8 when my last company was acquired. I always paid others before myself and gave myself just enough to cover immediate expenses.

I lived more frugally than a college student and tried to keep up appearances so nobody (ie. potential customers) would know the place I was in.

I was in survival mode for so long that it crushed my confidence. I questioned whether I had what it took and if my business was ever going to be real. 

I was hundreds of thousands of dollars in debt. I couldn’t give up because I didn't know how else to pay it off.

I grew resentful of my startup and everything associated with it. I couldn't feel success regardless of how big the wins were. Eventually, we dug our way out, got to profitability, and sold. 

I never fully got over the emotional trauma and habits I formed in that period. I still look at prices on a menu before the item, and opt for the cheapest glass of wine even when I have the means.

My last exit gave me the comfort to start eWebinar on my own terms. Finally, I experienced what it was like to make thoughtful decisions, proactive instead of reactive. The result was a better product, a better team, and a better me.

We think bootstrapping is hard because we lack resources to move fast and break things.

The hardest thing is to wake up drowning from trying to meet your own basic needs. Our lives are happening in parallel with our startups, yet we stop one to do the other. We need to go out, socialize, and date. We need to enjoy living to enjoy building.

Having been on both sides, I realized that it is vital to be mentally healthy to build a company, and that starts with getting paid so you have space to make good choices. You can't take care of others unless you take care of yourself. That’s just GOOD BUSINESS.

💡 I now believe founders should get paid first if they don’t have another source of income, and be the fastest ones to get paid the most.

I no longer believe in proving dedication by quitting your job before having something concrete. Floating yourself until your company can float you is strategic. I’ve seen companies and marriages fail because founders do not financially prioritize themselves.

I glorified the place I was in because I thought I needed to suffer in order to succeed. Instead of re-evaluating my business’ viability every couple months, I tried harder to push a bad business forward.

Plot twist: Sacrifice is not glorious and suffering is not an ingredient for success.

Business is hard, but it shouldn’t be THAT hard.
